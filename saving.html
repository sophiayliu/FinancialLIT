What are savings?

Savings is income that is not used. It is a method of setting money aside for further uses.

What is an emergency saving?

An emergency fund/saving is a sum of money that is set aside in case of unexpected expenses.

Common causes of emergency savings: unemployment, medical expenses, and unexpected troubles (home repairs, car repairs, etc).

Benefits of Having an Emergency Saving:

1) Lower stress levels : Knowing that you have an emergency fund to back you up in case of an emergency can significantly lower stress levels.

2) Encourages good financial habits : Being aware of your financial goals and plans will definitely help in preventing unwise purchases

Tips for an Efficient Emergency Fund:

1) Automate your savings - Set up a separate account for your emergency fund and have your employer directly deposit a certain amount of your pay into it. If you can’t see the money, you won’t be able to use it!

2) Lower your monthly spendings - Spending money wisely will leave you with more savings and funds for your emergency account.

3) Set reasonable goals - Have an overall goal for your emergency fund and set smaller goals to slowly work towards it. Emergency funds are NOT the same as savings; a small sum of money is taken from your savings in order to create your emergency funds. Therefore, you shouldn’t devote too much of your savings into your emergency fund!

What is a major purchase?

By definition, a major purchase is a purchase that exceeds $5,000. For teenagers and young adults however, major purchases can be anything from a new laptop, a car, and even a new pair of sneakers! Saving up such a large amount of money can often be difficult and challenging for many. Thankfully, there are many tips and strategies that are available for those looking to put in the effort!

What are retirement savings?

1. 401(k) plan: an employee-sponsored retirement plan that enables one to invest their annual contributions up to a specific limit and offers considerable tax benefits for after retirement

2. Traditional 401(k): contributions are deducted from an employee’s gross income

3. Roth 401(k): contributions are deduced from an employee’s after-tax income

Individual Retirement Arrangement (IRA): an account that is mostly opened and administered by individuals and that they can fund with either pre-tax or post-tax money

Traditional IRA: individuals pay no taxes on their contributions but pay taxes when they take the money out

Roth IRA: individuals pay taxes on their contributions, but they can withdraw money tax-free at retirement

What is a student loan?

A specific kind of loan intended to assist students in covering the costs of post-secondary education and related fees

Different options for federal student loans

Direct subsidized loans - loans that are available to college students based on financial need. Your grade determines the amount you qualify for. However, your parents’ income determines whether or not you are considered for the loan. Subsidized loans do not have interest while the student is in school; the interest begins accruing once the student graduates.

Direct unsubsidized loans: loans that are available to undergraduate, graduate, and professional students. Financial need does not dictate who these loans are available to. Interest accumulates over time.

Direct PLUS loans - loans that are available to graduate students and parents of dependent undergraduate students; these loans cover the costs that financial aid does not cover. If a student were in need of more money even after direct subsidized and unsubsidized loans, they could apply for a direct PLUS loan.

Direct consolidation loans: lets one combine several federal student loans with a loan servicer and interest rate. These loans increase the repayment term and lower the monthly bill, but they can increase the amount of interest paid.
