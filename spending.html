What is a budget?

Simply explained, a budget is a strategy for your money. You can devise a plan for your spending and saving by keeping track of your income and expenses. Instead of your money controlling you, you control your money. Develop habits to save, avoid financial crisis and maintain peace of mind.

How do you get started?

1. Start by creating a realistic monthly budget that includes all of your income and expenses.

2. Track your spending to ensure you are staying within your budget.

3. Identify areas where you can cut back on expenses, such as eating out or entertainment.

4. Prioritize your spending based on your financial goals and needs.

5. Review and adjust your budget regularly to ensure it remains relevant.

6. Take a look at financialLIT’s budget planner!

Tips on Spending:

Focus on needs vs. wants. Prioritize spending on necessary expenses such as rent, food, utilities, and transportation, before indulging in luxury expenses.

1. Follow the 50/20/30 rule! Divide your income into three categories, spending 50% on needs, 30% on wants, and 20% on savings.

2. Avoid impulse purchases. Plan for spending by creating shopping lists, price comparisons, and avoiding impulse buying.

3. Use credit cards responsibly. Keep track of your spending, pay your balance in full, and avoid accumulating high-interest credit card debt.

4. Consider long-term impacts. When considering a purchase, ask yourself if it aligns with your long-term financial goals, such as saving for a down payment on a house or retirement.

What is credit?

Credit is the ability to borrow money, and pay it back later, usually with interest.

That’s how a credit card works! It’s essentially a loan where you are borrowing money from the card issuer. You can then pay back the amount you borrowed (plus interest) over time. If you do not pay the full balance each month, you will be charged interest on the remaining balance. It is important to use credit cards responsibly and to only borrow what you can afford to pay back, in order to avoid accumulating debt and damaging your credit score.
